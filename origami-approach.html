DNA nanotechnology and DNA Origami

The past decade has taken the fundamental purpose of DNA as the genetic material of living organisms into remarkably sophisticated and versatile grounds. Taking the advantage of its programmability through molecular recognition (Watson-Crick base pairing) alongside advanced in silico design strategies, studies in DNA nanotechnology have exploited the exceptional opportunity to engineer and build highly structured synthetic materials and systems.[1, 2, 3]

When Paul Rothemund introduced his method, DNA origami, for folding single-stranded (ss), long DNA scaffold molecules into various desired two dimensional (2D) shapes by the assistance of short complementary oligonucleotides (staple strands), DNA and molecular self-assembly started to turn into inspirational tools for bottom-up fabrication in nanotechnology.[4] Since then, wide range of DNA nanostructures of custom 2D formations to much more complex, megadalton scale 3D assemblies have been reported for versatile objectives. [5, 6, 7, 8, 9]
