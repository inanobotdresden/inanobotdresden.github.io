Magentic Beads

Invented and defined first in 1976 as microbeads by John Ugelstad, today dynabeads are the spherical particles usually made up with a silicon core and covered with a layer of paramagnetic material.[1] Namely, they are not initially magnetized, but need an extra force to become magnets. Beads diameter typically lies in the range from 1 to 5 micrometer often found in many biological applications as magnetic nanoswimmers, e.g. isolation of small components such as proteins and peptides.

Dynabeads can be commercially purchased and functionalized afterwards. Every bead has a superparamagnetic material coated with a thin polymer shell composed of highly cross-linked polysterine with magnetic material precipitated in pores evenly distributed throughout the particle. The surface chemistry of dynabeads is hydrophilic which confers them low non-specific binding and covalent amide bond formation to proteins.[ 2, 3 ]
